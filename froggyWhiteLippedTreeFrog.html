White-Lipped Tree Frog

No hidden connotation behind the name

Care Level: Intermediate


Price Range: ~15$ (USD)


Lifespan: ~10 yrs


Diet: Crickets, mealworms, other insects


Compatibility: Able to live with other breeds

Profile Overview

The white-lipped tree frog is given its name due to the white line drawn out at its bottom lip (and hopefully for that one reason only). It also goes by the name, giant tree frog, since it is rather large in size (can reach 5+ inches- that's large for a jumper), and it can be found in many tree-abundant areas. It is very far from being endangered due to its ability to adapt to various climates and environments.

Handling of the white-lipped tree frog should be minimal as it does get stressed out easily from human contact and can contract an illness/disease from excessive contact. The reason why the care level is set to intermediate is because of its inreased need of resources. White-lips need more food and a larger habitat due to its size, making it more expensive (than other frogs) to take care of in the long run. It is almost impossible to differentiate between male and female white-lipped tree frogs, which may become problematic if you are planning on breeding them.

Environment Overview

  • Capacity: 15" x 15" x 20" (minimum)
  • Temperature: ~ 24℃
  • Humidity: 60%- 70%

The giant tree frog originates from Australia, Indonesia, and Papua New Guinea. These frogs can be found in gardens, parks, forests, and fields. Basically, the white-lipped tree frog is not picky with regards to its living environment. What's necessary for it is space for it to move/jump around. An aboreal environment is good height-wise, but a mix with a mutiple-zone environment is also a good choice because it provides more space to it, along with giving it options on where it can play around. Make sure the tanks have climbable decor, since they are aboreal animals. For jungle creatures like this one, UVB lighting is needed in order to replicate their natural habitat.

Giant tree frogs don't necessarily need companions, but it's always nice to have a friend, right? These frogs are relatively docile by nature, and thus can get along with other species of frogs. Be sure to research whether the other species of frog are compatible with white-lipped tree frogs.

All the information presented here is just an overview. It's recommended that you do futher research into these species of frogs if you are interested in adopting them.
